Preface to the series

What is this series? From an interview with Erikson:

“The genre is epic fantasy, although the series could also be characterized as a dramatized history on an alternate world, one where magic works and the gods are active in the mortal realm. But this is not simply a history of nations, kings, queens, empires and tyrants. Rather, the series explores life from every social stratum, among many cultures and civilizations; from petty thieves to soldiers (on both sides of the conflict) to barkeeps to sorcerers, priests, demigods and gods.

Another take on answering your question: the Malazan series recounts a period of history centered on one particular empire (the Malazan Empire) during a time of expansion and conquest, and how this empire becomes instrumental in fundamental changes to the world (once the gods weigh in).”

Preface to GotM Redux that may not be in your edition and is worth reading

Gardens of the Moon was originally written as a movie script, then converted into a novel in 1992. While he was shopping around for a publisher, I’m assuming Erikson graduated from the Iowa Writer’s Workshop that greatly improved his writing. Eventually the series was picked up by Bantam UK and published seven years later in 1999. Over the next 12 years, Erikson would write and publish novels almost on a yearly basis (Book 6 and Book 10 were the ones that took 2 years).

Quick Tips

Malazan is pronounced (ma-LA-zin). It’s a national descriptor for an empire founded in Malaz City. The word is similar to saying “American”, “German”, “Italian”, “Roman Empire”, etc.

Avoid using Google or using the Malazan Wiki because it’s very easy to see huge spoilers just in the search bar or in fan art.

Post in /r/Malazan any questions and we’ll gladly answer. We are a very friendly, albeit fanatical, community that likes talking to newbies.

A lot of names get thrown at you that you might not ever see again, it’s hard discerning what you actually need to remember going forward. Words bold red are things I personally chose to highlight to stay in your head in hopes you’ll get to recognize them in future books. They’re not super important to the plot, just a little consistency in remembering where you saw that name from. If you see a name you think it’s important and don’t see in this ppt, then it’s something I didn’t bother remembering.

Erikson doesn’t hold your hand? No info dumps?

As you read, you’ll feel as lost as most of the characters you follow.

There are many POV’s that would typically be protagonists or major characters you would expect to follow, like Anomander Rake. However, Erikson chooses to psychically distance the reader from those characters. Instead you follow the sidekicks, the confidants, the soldiers with their boots on the ground, the gods entangled by their decisions, and those that have to carry out or deal with the fallout of their choices.

There are actually info dumps, they just come several books later in the form of the characters making the discoveries themselves. You aren’t given any expositions that explain the world, characters, magic, or gods. You experience events and conversations, and form your own idea of how things work bit by bit. The questions you have during the series are the same questions that the characters have themselves. Part of the fun reading the series is the satisfaction you have when you make conclusions over several books and they match those info dumps.

Why I made this

Although I instantly fell in love with Gardens of the Moon, other people struggle with getting through it. A lot of people end up dropping the series before they could finish. However, the second book Deadhouse Gates, was written a decade after Gardens of the Moon and is much more improved in writing quality. Most people find their footing in Deadhouse Gates and began to enjoy the series then. So, I made this to help new readers get over the entry barrier that GotM can be to get them to DG.

In a nutshell, the series follows the Malazan Empire in an age of its imperialism where they are expanding and conquering other lands for economic wealth and political power. In Gardens of the Moon, you will follow an elite Malazan force, the Bridgeburners, as they conquer the free cities of the Genabackis continent.

F.A.Q.

If you still have any lingering questions after finishing the book and this guide, leave a comment on the Google Slide itself or on the subreddit threads.

The acorn was not intended to be a seed for the Azath House. The Azath House grew to imprison the acorn and Raest’s body.

Why would a T’lan Imass want to free a Jaghut Tyrant?

Tool is the last of his clan, so if Raest dominated Tool his influence wouldn’t reach any other T’lan Imass. Tool is expendable.

Raest was imprisoned, but was still vulnerable to someone discovering his barrow and releasing him. So Tool went and did it himself, and despite saying he wasn’t going to get himself involved he attacked Raest immediately and fought his Finnest so that the Azath could imprison him

Why the hell did the Malazan Empire want to release the Jaghut Tyrant and why did the Bridgeburners let Adjunct Lorn into Darujhistan despite anticipating betrayal?

They all planned for Anomander Rake to be forced into defending the city (and the world) from the Jaghut Tyrant. They expected Rake to win, and end up weakened making Darujhistan vulnerable for the Empire to conquer. The Bridgeburners let Adjunct Lorn enter Darujhistan because they correctly assumed the Tyrant would target her (since she carried the acorn-shaped Finnest)

In the early editions, Orfantal was a female but was corrected in later editions to be male

The book began as a movie script in 1991, and then Erikson converted it into novel form and finally published it in 1999 which may explain why it feels different. From Deadhouse Gate and onwards, Erikson had written the series successively in polished novel form
